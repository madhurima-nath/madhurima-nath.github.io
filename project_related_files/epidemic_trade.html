Epidemic & Trade Network Applications

Doctoral research, Virginia Tech, 2013–2018, supervised by Prof. Stephen Eubank. Moore-Shannon network reliability — a polynomial that encodes both network structure and the dynamics of a process running on it — is applied here to two systems: human contact networks, where it reveals how standard graph models fail to reproduce epidemic dynamics; and international food trade networks, where it identifies which groups of countries share vulnerability to a crop-borne invasive species. The full dissertation is available online.

SIR Dynamics on Contact Networks

Infectious diseases spread through contact networks — graphs in which each person is a node and each potential transmission route an edge. Under the SIR model, each person is susceptible \((S)\), infectious \((I)\), or recovered \((R)\). An infectious person transmits to each susceptible contact independently with probability \(x\). The central question is whether networks constructed to match observed local statistics also reproduce the epidemic dynamics observed on the real network.

N-Terminal reliability under SIR dynamics

\(R(x;\,\mathcal{G},D_\text{SIR},\mathcal{P}_N)\) gives the probability that infecting a single randomly chosen node leads to an outbreak involving at least \(N\) people, where \(x\) is the per-edge transmission probability. It is a finite-degree polynomial in \(x\) that captures both network topology and epidemic dynamics in a single quantity.

The observed network is school 86 — a friendship network drawn from Wave I of the Add Health study, a US national longitudinal survey of adolescent social networks. It covers two high schools with 1,460 students and 974 mutual friendship links. Against this, ensembles of exponential random graph models (ERGMs) — statistical models that generate random networks constrained to match specified properties — were constructed to match school 86 on edge count, node attributes, clustering, and a geometric weighting of shared partnerships. The Faux Magnolia network, a well-known ERGM fit in the literature based on the same data, serves as the primary comparison.

Comparing \(R(x;\alpha)\) across all networks shows that school 86 is significantly more resistant to large outbreaks than any of the ERGMs, even those that closely match its degree distribution, clustering coefficients, and centrality measures. At matched transmission probability, ERGMs overestimate infection numbers by up to 50%. When transmission probability is re-calibrated so that all networks have the same epidemic potential, systematic differences in peak infection height and outbreak duration persist. A less constrained random network, re-calibrated to the same epidemic potential, performs as well as or better than Faux Magnolia at reproducing school 86 dynamics — indicating that over-constraining to local statistics introduces spurious global structure.

The conclusion is that attack rate depends on global topological features not captured by degree distribution or clustering. Standard summary statistics are insufficient to characterise epidemic dynamics, and observed contact network data remains essential when designing and evaluating intervention strategies such as vaccination.

Invasive Species Spread in Food Trade Networks

The global food system depends on long-distance trade in agricultural commodities, which creates pathways for invasive species and crop pests to propagate between countries. This work applies Moore-Shannon network reliability to identify contagion clusters in international food trade networks — groups of countries with shared vulnerability, where a pest entering one country substantially increases the likelihood of spread to the others.

The case study is Tuta absoluta (South American tomato leafminer), an invasive pest that has spread from its native South America to most of Europe, Africa, and West, Central, and South Asia. Trade data for the four crops of the Solanaceae family — tomato, potato, eggplant, and pepper — comes from the FAO (Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations) Detailed Trade Matrices at country level, covering 2005–2013. Edge weights are set to the maximum of reported export and import volumes between each country pair, representing a worst-case scenario for contagion risk.

Contagion clusters are identified using a reliability-based algorithm that removes trade edges in order of their marginal contribution to network reliability, until no strongly connected component exceeds a specified size. This ranks trade routes by structural importance to the contagion process. The minimum cut needed to disconnect each cluster, and its total incoming and outgoing trade volumes, characterise its vulnerability and influence in the wider network.

A stable European cluster — Portugal, Netherlands, Belgium, France, and Spain — appears in the tomato trade network across all years. A distinct North American cluster (USA, Mexico, Canada) is identified separately. Clusters form primarily among geographically adjacent countries with strong bilateral trade, and the structure is robust: stable across different transmission probability values and different trade volume quantisation levels. As total trade volumes grow between 2005 and 2013, clusters expand and fewer countries remain outside network effects. Forecasting contagion propagation across identified clusters achieved approximately 96% precision.
