Epidemic & Trade Network Applications

Doctoral research, Virginia Tech, 2013–2019, supervised by Prof. Stephen Eubank. Moore-Shannon network reliability is applied here to two classes of networked systems: human contact networks, where it measures epidemic potential and reveals structural limitations of graph models; and international food trade networks, where it identifies contagion clusters and vulnerability to crop-specific disruptions. The full dissertation is available through VTechWorks.

SIR Dynamics on Contact Networks

Contact networks shape how diseases spread through populations. Network models and simulations allow researchers to explore disease dynamics under different assumptions without requiring complete real-world data. This work compares SIR epidemic outcomes on observed contact networks from the Add Health dataset with outcomes on exponential random graph models (ERGMs) constructed to match the same summary statistics, using Moore-Shannon network reliability to measure epidemic potential across the full range of transmission probability.

SIR model

The SIR model divides a population of \(N\) into susceptible \(S\), infected \(I\), and recovered \(R\) compartments, with transmission rate \(\beta\) and recovery rate \(\gamma\):

\[\frac{dS}{dt} = -\frac{\beta}{N}\,SI, \qquad \frac{dI}{dt} = \frac{\beta}{N}\,SI - \gamma I, \qquad \frac{dR}{dt} = \gamma I\]

An outbreak grows when the basic reproduction number \(\mathcal{R}_0 = \beta/\gamma > 1\). On a contact network, however, epidemic dynamics depend on structure as well as parameters — a dependence that aggregate statistics such as degree distribution and clustering coefficients fail to fully capture. Network reliability summarises this directly, giving the probability \(R_\alpha(p)\) that at least \(\alpha N\) individuals are infected when each contact transmits with probability \(p = \beta/(\beta + \gamma)\).

Networks generated from ERGMs overestimated infection numbers by up to 50% compared to observed networks, despite matching degree distribution and clustering coefficients. Reliability analysis revealed that temporal patterns also differed systematically: peak infection timing, outbreak magnitude, and duration all varied between real and modelled networks. Across multiple Monte Carlo runs, these differences persisted. Standard network statistics fail to capture structural features critical to epidemic dynamics, and observed contact network data remains essential when evaluating control strategies.

Food Trade Networks & Pestilence Distribution

Global food systems are increasingly interconnected through long-distance trade, creating vulnerability to disruptions from climate events, pests, and contamination. This research applies Moore-Shannon network reliability community detection to international food trade networks to identify clusters of countries whose trade patterns create mutual dependencies in how disruptions propagate.

Analysis of UN Comtrade data for Solanaceae crops (potato, tomato) shows that contagion clusters form primarily between geographically adjacent countries with strong bilateral trade relationships. Domestic US food freight networks — cereal and mixed agricultural flows — reveal even more pronounced geographic clustering, with communities concentrated in specific regions. The hierarchical structure of communities shifts with the reliability threshold: larger clusters break into progressively smaller, more cohesive subgroups as the threshold increases. Forecasting the propagation of contagion conditions across identified clusters achieved approximately 96% precision.

As global trade intensifies, clusters grow larger and fewer countries remain outside network effects. Identifying vulnerable clusters enables targeted surveillance, highlights critical trade routes requiring monitoring, and indicates where supply chain diversification or biosecurity measures would be most effective in reducing systemic vulnerability.
