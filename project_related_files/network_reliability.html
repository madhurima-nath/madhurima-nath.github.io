Network Reliability: Theory & Computation

Doctoral research, Virginia Tech, 2013–2019, supervised by Prof. Stephen Eubank. The central object is the Moore-Shannon network reliability polynomial — a framework, originally developed for circuit analysis, for computing the probability that a graph dynamical system satisfies a desired property. These two sections cover the computational foundations and the extension to Ising model partition function estimation. The full dissertation is available through VTechWorks.

Computational Frameworks for Network Reliability

Moore-Shannon network reliability addresses the following question: given a graph dynamical system, what is the probability that it satisfies a desired property? This is equivalent to probabilistic satisfiability (PSAT), which is #P-complete — meaning no efficient exact algorithm is known for the general case. The reliability polynomial expresses this probability as a function of the per-edge success probability \(p\), but direct evaluation requires summing over an exponentially large collection of subgraphs.

the reliability polynomial

For a graph \(G = (V, E)\), the reliability polynomial is the probability that the active edges satisfy a target property (e.g., connectivity), when each edge is active independently with probability \(p\):

\[R(p) \;=\; \sum_{\substack{A \subseteq E \\ A\;\text{satisfies target}}} p^{|A|}(1-p)^{|E \setminus A|}\]

Two Taylor series bypass exhaustive enumeration — one from the low-\(p\) limit (built from minimal struts, the smallest edge-sets that satisfy the target) and one from the high-\(p\) limit (built from minimal cuts, the smallest edge-sets whose removal makes the target fail):

\[R_{\mathrm{low}}(p) = \sum_{k=m_+}^{D} a_k\, p^k \qquad\qquad R_{\mathrm{high}}(p) = 1 - \sum_{k=m_-}^{D} b_k\,(1-p)^k\]

The two expansions are merged by Bernstein polynomial interpolation. With degree \(n = D_+ + D_-\):

\[R(p) \;\approx\; \sum_{k=0}^{n} c_k \binom{n}{k} p^k(1-p)^{n-k}\]

The coefficients \(c_k \in [0,1]\) are constrained to be non-decreasing, enforcing unitarity and monotonicity. As the expansion depths \(D_+\) and \(D_-\) increase, the approximation converges to the exact polynomial.

When minimal struts and cuts are not known in advance, Monte Carlo sampling identifies them from the graph structure. Two parameters — \(S\) (number of samples) and \(D\) (expansion depth) — control approximation quality. The method provides provable upper and lower bounds on the error: these bounds are tight, meaning problems exist that saturate each one, but they can be systematically narrowed by increasing \(S\) and \(D\). The framework applies to directed, weighted, multiplexed, and time-dependent networks, and handles both homogeneous systems (uniform \(p\) across edges) and heterogeneous ones (distinct probability per edge).

Ising Model Partition Function Estimation

The Ising model partition function — central to understanding phase transitions and critical phenomena in magnetic systems — is computationally intractable in general, particularly when an external magnetic field is present. Standard Monte Carlo methods converge poorly near critical points, and Wang-Landau approaches estimate only the univariate density of states, which does not extend to field-dependent systems. The key insight is that the Ising partition function is exactly a bivariate Moore-Shannon reliability polynomial, once spin configurations are counted through the joint density of states \(g(k,v)\). This separates structural factors from dynamical ones and opens the Bernstein interpolation machinery to the problem.

Ising Hamiltonian and partition function

The Ising model on a graph \(G = (V,E)\) with exchange coupling \(J\) and external field \(B\):

\[\mathcal{H} = -J \sum_{\langle i,j \rangle \in E} \sigma_i \sigma_j \;-\; B \sum_{i \in V} \sigma_i, \qquad \sigma_i \in \{-1,+1\}\]

The partition function factors through \(g(k,v)\) — the number of spin configurations with exactly \(k\) anti-aligned bonds and total magnetization \(v\):

\[Z(\beta) \;=\; \sum_{\{\sigma\}} e^{-\beta\mathcal{H}} \;=\; e^{\beta J|E|} \sum_{k,v} g(k,v)\, e^{-2\beta Jk}\, e^{\beta Bv}\]

Setting \(x = e^{-2\beta J}\) and \(y = e^{\beta B}\), this is the bivariate reliability polynomial \(R(x,y)\):

\[Z(\beta) \;=\; e^{\beta J|E|} \cdot R(x,y), \qquad R(x,y) = \sum_{k,v} g(k,v)\, x^k y^v\]

Low- and high-temperature expansions of \(Z\) serve as the two Taylor series for the Bernstein interpolation. The joint density \(g(k,v)\) is estimated using a parallel spin-exchange Markov chain Monte Carlo scheme that requires no inter-processor communication.

The Bernstein representation converts the infinite-series expansions into finite-degree polynomials evaluable via de Casteljau's algorithm, reducing time complexity and enabling stable evaluation across the full temperature range — including near critical points where standard Monte Carlo methods stall. The parallel scheme is suited to cluster computing and applies to large, irregular graphs where no closed-form analytical solution exists. This enables practical study of field-dependent phase transitions in systems too structurally complex for exact methods.
