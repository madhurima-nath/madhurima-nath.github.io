Dissertation Overview

Moore-Shannon network reliability serves as a global statistic to explore the behaviour of diffusive processes on networks. It depends on both network topology and the dynamics of the process, giving the probability that the system reaches a desired configuration. This work applies the reliability formalism to three key areas: modelling epidemic outbreaks on contact networks, identifying vulnerabilities in international trade, and optimising partition function estimation for the Ising model. Epidemic Modelling on Contact Networks

Used network reliability to study how network topology affects epidemic outbreak sensitivity. Simulated Susceptible-Infected-Recovered (SIR) dynamics on real contact networks and statistical graph models, demonstrating that network reliability captures structural effects beyond traditional measures.

Contagion Spread in Global Food Trade Networks

Utilised network reliability to identify dynamics-induced contagion clusters and vulnerabilities within international food trade networks using United Nations (UN) Comtrade data. Forecasted the impact of mitigating contagion conditions with approximately 96% precision.

Network Reliability on Ising Model

Established that the network reliability polynomial is analogous to the Ising partition function. Used Bernstein polynomial interpolation to estimate partition functions from low- and high-temperature expansions, reducing time complexity from O (n2) to O (n).

Research Themes

Computational Frameworks for Network Reliability

Applied the Moore-Shannon network reliability formalism (traditionally used for electronic circuit analysis) to biological and social network systems. This established a theoretical framework connecting electrical engineering concepts to network epidemiology and complex systems analysis. The reliability polynomial $R(x; G, D, P)$ calculates the probability that a graph dynamical system satisfies a desired property under specific stochastic dynamics.

Computational approaches were developed to evaluate the formalism using Bernstein basis functions as a polynomial basis set, providing convergence guarantees for sequential design and model selection in discrete finite systems. This framework addresses NP-hard computational problems by combining Monte Carlo simulations with weak- and strong-coupling perturbative expansions. The methodology enables the construction of tight upper and lower bounds on approximation error for complex probabilistic satisfiability problems on large graphs.

SIR dynamics on contact networks vs statistical graph models

Contact networks shape how diseases spread through populations. Network models and simulations enable researchers to explore disease dynamics under different scenarios and conditions without requiring exact real-world data. Disease outcomes on observed contact networks from the Add Health dataset were compared with those on exponential random graph models (ERGMs) designed to match the same network statistics, using Moore-Shannon network reliability to measure epidemic potential.

Networks generated from ERGMs overestimated infection numbers by up to 50% compared to observed networks, despite matching degree distribution and clustering coefficients. Moore-Shannon network reliability analysis revealed that temporal patterns of disease spread also differed systematically between real and modelled networks, with variations in the timing and magnitude of peak infections and in how long outbreaks persisted. Across multiple Monte Carlo simulation runs, these differences persisted consistently. The findings indicate that network models fail to capture structural features critical to epidemic dynamics.

Attack rates depend on complex structural properties beyond the statistics typically used to specify network models. Estimates of intervention effectiveness derived from approximate networks cannot reliably generalise to real contact networks. Using observed network data remains essential when evaluating epidemic control strategies. Moore-Shannon network reliability serves as a tool for identifying dynamically important differences between networks that standard statistical measures miss.

Food Trade Networks & Pestilence Distribution

Global food systems are increasingly interconnected through long-distance trade, creating vulnerability to disruptions such as climate events, pests, and contamination. This research applies Moore-Shannon network reliability community detection to international food trade networks to identify clusters of countries whose trade patterns create mutual dependencies in how disruptions propagate.

Analysis of international trade networks for Solanaceae crops shows that clusters form primarily between geographically adjacent countries with strong bilateral trade relationships. Domestic US food networks ‐ cereal and mixed agricultural freight flows ‐ reveal even more pronounced geographic clustering, with communities concentrated in specific regions. The hierarchical structure of communities shows that as thresholds change, larger communities break into progressively smaller, more cohesive groups.

As global trade intensifies over time, clusters grow larger and fewer countries remain isolated from network effects. This trend has direct implications for food security policy: identifying vulnerable clusters enables targeted surveillance priorities, highlights critical trade routes requiring monitoring, and indicates where supply chain diversification or biosecurity measures would be most effective in reducing systemic vulnerability.

Ising Model Partition Function Estimation

An algorithm was developed using Moore-Shannon network reliability to estimate energy states of an interacting magnetic system modeled on 1D and 2D lattices. By treating spin flips as a diffusive process on a graph dynamical system, established that the reliability polynomial is analogous to the Ising partition function.

By reformulating the Ising model through the network reliability framework, an equivalence was established that enabled the transfer of statistical physics computational methods to network science. A parallel Markov-chain Monte Carlo scheme was implemented for estimating the joint density of states, reducing time complexity from O (n2) to O (n).
