Dissertation Overview

Moore-Shannon network reliability provides a robust statistical framework for investigating diffusive processes on complex networks. By accounting for the interplay between underlying topology and stochastic dynamics, this metric calculates the probability of a system attaining specific target configurations. This work applies the reliability formalism to three key areas: modelling epidemic outbreaks on contact networks, identifying vulnerabilities in global trade, and optimising partition function estimation in statistical physics. Epidemic Modelling on Contact Networks

Used network reliability to study how network topology affects epidemic outbreak sensitivity. Simulated Susceptible-Infected-Recovered (SIR) dynamics on real contact networks and statistical graph models, demonstrating that network reliability captures structural effects beyond traditional measures.

Contagion Spread in Global Food Trade Networks

Utilised network reliability to identify dynamics-induced contagion clusters and vulnerabilities within international food trade networks using United Nations (UN) Comtrade data. This methodology incorporates Moore-Shannon Network Reliability – Community Detection (MSNR-CD) to identify strongly connected components critical for propagation. The impact of mitigation strategies was forecasted with approximately 96% precision.

Network Reliability on Ising Model

Established that the network reliability polynomial is analogous to the Ising partition function. Used Bernstein polynomial interpolation to estimate partition functions from low- and high-temperature expansions, reducing time complexity from O (n2) to O (n).

Research Themes

Susceptible-Infected-Recovered dynamics on contact networks vs statistical graph models

Simulated real-world epidemic outbreak and intervention scenarios on the National Longitudinal Study of Adolescent to Adult Health (Add Health) dataset using network reliability. Compared Susceptible-Infected-Recovered (SIR) dynamics on observed contact networks against four classes of exponential random graph models (ERGMs) to determine whether standard network statistics suffice to characterise epidemic outcomes.

The analysis demonstrated that models matching local network statistics can significantly overestimate infection numbers - sometimes by as much as 50%. This revealed that the attack rate depends on a complex mixture of structural properties beyond degree distribution, clustering, and centrality measures.

Furthermore, Birnbaum importance measures were applied to quantify individual edge contributions to epidemic potential, enabling the identification of critical connections for targeted mitigation strategies.

International Food Trade & Pestilence Distribution

Vulnerabilities within international trade were identified by tracking the contagion risk of invasive species across commodity-specific networks. Using commodity-specific data from the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) and United Nations (UN) Comtrade databases, directed weighted networks were constructed for commodities such as tomatoes, potatoes, and cereals between 2005-2013 to map how pathogens, such as Tuta absoluta, could spread through international trade networks.

Advanced statistical approaches for community detection (Moore-Shannon Network Reliability – Community Detection, MSNR-CD) were developed to analyse these large-scale networks. This work introduced the concept of contagion clusters to identify strongly connected components (SCCs) which are critical for the propagation of contagion within the network. By analysing network topology and stochastic dynamics, the framework achieved approximately 96% precision in forecasting impact of mitigation strategies under various contagion scenarios, identifying crucial links for preventing transnational pest expansion.

Computational Frameworks for Network Reliability

The Moore-Shannon network reliability formalism was extended to biological and social systems to establish a theoretical framework for structural analysis. The reliability polynomial, R(x), represents the probability that a graph dynamical system satisfies a desired global property under specific stochastic dynamics.

Novel computational approaches were developed using Bernstein basis functions as a polynomial basis set. By treating the reliability polynomial as a kernel, convergence guarantees were established for sequential design and statistical model selection in discrete finite systems. This framework addresses NP-hard computational problems by combining Monte Carlo simulations with weak- and strong-coupling perturbative expansions. The methodology enables the construction of tight upper and lower bounds on approximation error for complex probabilistic satisfiability problems on large graphs.

Ising Model Partition Function Estimation

An algorithm was developed using Moore-Shannon network reliability to estimate energy states of an interacting magnetic system modeled on 1D and 2D lattices. By treating spin flips as a diffusive process on a graph dynamical system, established that the reliability polynomial is analogous to the Ising partition function.

By reformulating the Ising model through the network reliability framework, an equivalence was established that enabled the transfer of statistical physics computational methods to network science. A parallel Markov-chain Monte Carlo scheme was implemented for estimating the joint density of states, reducing time complexity from O (n2) to O (n).
