Network Infrastructure - something

A statistical method based on device radio frequency (RF) metrics was developed to improve the approach for network infrastructure maintenance, for a telecommunications client facing escalating operational costs from their reactive service visits. The traditional approach relied on registering a service visit to address to multiple customer complaints. This was not only expensive but also damaged customer satisfaction through prolonged service disruptions. A portion of operational expenses could be reduced if some repeated service visits and support calls could be decreased through proactive maintenance.
The proposed solution monitors the device health of customers and proactively identifies possible at-risk network infrastructure in a smaller geo-location within a region. A comprehensive risk scoring algorithm was engineered using the hourly data available for the key RF performance metrics (indicators of device health):

  • signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)
  • downstream receiving power
  • upstream transmitting power
    • The risk score plotted against six months of historical service visits and call rates validate the correlation that a higher risk score leads to more calls and subsequently more service visits. Specifically, six months of historical data were analyzed to identify ranges where call rates showed monotonically increasing or decreasing relationships with RF metrics. By focusing on the 5th to 95th percentile range and excluding the extreme values at both ends, the analysis established reliable minimum and maximum thresholds for each RF metric, creating a foundation for standardization. The technical heart of the solution was a weighted scoring formula that transformed raw RF measurements into actionable risk scores. For each metric, a standardized value was calculated using the formula: $S_i = \frac{|x_i - x_{min}|}{x_{max} - x_{min}}$ where $x_i$ represented the hourly metric value from device data. The weight for each metric was determined by measuring the slope of linear regression between call rates and the normalized metric, represented as $w_i = \frac{\Delta\text{CallRate}}{\Delta\text{NormalizedMetric}_i}$. The final risk score was calculated as $\text{RiskScore} = \sum_{i=1}^3 w_i \times S_i$, effectively capturing each metric's relative contribution to service issues. By processing this scoring model through custom SQL stored procedures scheduled for daily execution, the system continuously updated risk profiles across the network. The most innovative aspect was implementing a DBSCAN clustering algorithm that identified geographical concentrations of high-risk customers, enabling targeted maintenance of problematic network nodes rather than addressing individual customer issues. Initial validation in Region 1 demonstrated compelling results, identifying potential monthly reductions of approximately 4,500 customer calls. The methodology successfully flagged known problematic nodes with high precision, confirming the approach's validity. Working closely with field technicians, feedback mechanisms were established to continuously refine the risk scoring model and improve prediction accuracy. This collaborative validation process was crucial for gaining organizational buy-in and ensuring the solution addressed real-world network issues. The final production solution enabled the transformation from reactive to proactive maintenance scheduling based on statistical risk indicators. Operations teams received daily reports highlighting emerging high-risk clusters requiring preventive intervention, dramatically shifting resource allocation from emergency response to planned maintenance. This not only reduced costs but improved network reliability metrics and customer satisfaction scores by addressing potential failures before they impacted service. The solution's ROI extended beyond direct cost savings to include improved customer retention and reduced churn in previously problematic service areas.
